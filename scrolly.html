Atelier

Mallory Johnson

Atelier is loosely inspired by manga artists 市川春子 (Ichikawa Haruko) and 白浜鴎 (Shirahama Kamome), entomologist Adolphe Philippe Millot, and the color palettes used by the US National Parks Service.

I often find my cartography work inspired by simple color palettes with minimal textures and visible hues. I wanted to divert from what I usually prefer in a map to do something inspired by what I prefer in the art media I consume; textured and stylized.

The visual inspiration foir this map follows a muted color palette, with greens and shades of browns as the primary colors. The US National Park Service maps follow a very similar color palette, so I also used some National Park maps as a point of reference when choosing colors.

This map is not meant to be overly utilitarian; roads and points-of-interest are not visible until significantly zoomed in. The most important features in the map, the texture of the land and the natural areas, are visible at a further distance.

At a distance, many details such as smaller roads and labels are excluded to keep the map from appearing busy. As you zoom in, land-use areas (parks, city boundaries) fade in but are in colors which keep them in the background rather than hopping to the foreground.

Areas with variation in topography will have more texture than flatter areas. For example, Thunder Bay in Canada has a good amount of variation.

However, even flat areas like the center of Kansas, USA, have a fair amount of texture since the range of values for the elevation model adjusts to values present in the map view.

Other textures, such as bathymetry, will only be visible at smaller scales where the ocean is visible.

While certain features, such as contour lines will add more texture in at a very large scale.
